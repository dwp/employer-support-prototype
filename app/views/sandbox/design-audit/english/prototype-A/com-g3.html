This section: having conversations with your employee

Talk to your employee

Start a conversation with your employee as soon as you can. Let your employee know you support them and give them time if they need it.

How to start

Some people might be reluctant to start a conversation, so it's okay to make the first move. You should:

  • listen and be open to what they say
  • be patient and let the conversation develop gradually
  • notice the words they prefer to use and use those words yourself
  • let them know you understand that they might need changes to their job or workplace

What to keep in mind

Your employee will not want to be patronised or treated differently, but it’s okay to take some steps to support their unique needs.

All employees are legally protected from discrimination in the workplace. This duty of care comes under the Equality Act 2010 (opens in new tab).

You should avoid:

  • making assumptions about how their condition might affect them
  • using terms like ‘suffering from’ which suggests they are a victim
  • assuming they will need your help (it's okay if they decline, but remember to check again later)

Talk regularly

Many conditions change over time, so it's important to talk regularly to make sure any support is still working.

You should agree how often you will talk together. Depending on your situation, once a week or once a month might work.

Keep a record of discussions

Make a note of the dates of all conversations you have and what was discussed. This can be a helpful reminder of what you agreed to do.

Keep health information private. If you need to share it with colleagues, get your employee's consent first.

Read about supporting disabled workers and workers with a long term health conditions on the HSE website (opens in new tab).
