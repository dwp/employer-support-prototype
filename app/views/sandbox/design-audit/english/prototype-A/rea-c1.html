Making changes to ways of working

Changes to hours, shift patterns or policies can help your employee do their best at work. These changes could include allowing an employee to start their day later or encouraging regular screen breaks.

Consider and agree each change with your employee and review these regularly. Changes are sometimes temporary as health conditions can have different needs over time.

Checking policies

You should refer to any policies you have in place around absences, health and disability, or health and safety. Both temporary and permanent changes could support them.

Examples of changes to ways of working

These examples might not be relevant to your situation but you might still be able to use some of the ideas and processes. These might be scheduling regular catch-ups or putting a temporary buddy system in place

Find out how a gym owner supported a member of staff with a temporary mental health condition.

An employee was verbally attacked by a customer at the gym.

Following this incident:

  • the employee felt nervous about coming in for shifts
  • colleagues noticed the employee was not as welcoming or chatty to customers as usual
  • the employee didn't feel comfortable leading exercise classes alone

What happened first

The gym owner noticed a change in the employee and set up a meeting to discuss possible support. The employer researched mental health awareness training to help them understand how best to support staff if this situation were to happen again.

At the meeting the employer suggested:

  • the employee takes a short break from customer-facing tasks
  • all staff work in pairs when opening and locking up the gym
  • staff who take classes alone are to have a buddy – a colleague who joins the class as an observer

They agreed to meet again a couple of weeks after the changes had been put in place to see if they're helping the employee manage their anxiety.

What happened next

The employee felt comfortable running classes and agreed to try leading a session on their own in a couple of weeks. They're also working one shift a week on the front desk with a buddy for a trial period.

What's happening now

They now have regular meetings to talk about any issues and to discuss and agree on any changes needed. The employer makes sure that notes from their meetings are kept private.

Find out how a shop owner supported their employee to manage their menopause symptoms in the workplace.

An employee at a busy shop was experiencing menopause symptoms.

They found that:

  • it was difficult to concentrate in the noisy and busy environment
  • they felt uncomfortable and embarrassed during a hot flush
  • their anxiety increased when dealing with customers
  • they got upset if they forgot stock and pricing changes

What happened first

The manager noticed the change in the employee. They set up a meeting with the employee to find out how they could support them.

The employee told their manager that they were going through menopause. They discussed how the symptoms were affecting the employee’s role and the available options to help manage the symptoms at work. They agree to meet again in a week.

What happened next

They agreed that:

  • for a trial period, a colleague will cover customer-facing duties when the employee needs to freshen up or take a quick break
  • the manager will research menopause symptoms to better understand how to support their employee

What's happening now

The employee and manager have regular catch-ups. They discuss how the employee is managing the menopause, if the temporary changes are helping and if any other support is needed.
