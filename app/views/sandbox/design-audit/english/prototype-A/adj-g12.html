Putting workplace changes in place

What is reasonable?

What is ‘reasonable’ will depend on your business. It would be helpful for you to consider the extent to which any change could help overcome the disadvantage faced by your employee.

You can consider things such as:

  • how practical and effective it is to make an adjustment
  • the cost of the adjustment
  • the size of your business
  • the resources available to you
  • the benefit to your employee

As well as possible changes to your employee’s role or work environment, you could change the way they work. For example, some people prefer to do different tasks or fewer hours when their condition is worse.

If an employee uses a wheelchair and you do not have space for a lift, you will not need to change premises. But you would need to consider other ways for them to be able to do their job, such as working from somewhere else.

See more examples of changes on the Equality and Human Rights Commission website (opens in new tab)

Examples of reasonable adjustments

The changes you make will depend on your employee’s needs. For example, you could consider:

  • providing a closer car parking space for an employee who struggles to walk longer distances
  • more breaks for an employee with fatigue
  • reducing public-facing tasks if that environment makes an employee's mental health condition worse
  • giving verbal, as well as written, instructions for tasks could help an employee with a cognitive disability

For more information and guidance check the following websites:

  • reasonable adjustments for workers with disabilities or health conditions on GOV.UK (opens in new tab)
  • workplace adjustments guidance on Equality and Human Rights Commission (EHRC) (opens in new tab)
  • What are reasonable adjustments on AbilityNet website (opens in new tab)

If cost is one of the reasons you are unable to make changes

If adjustments are more expensive than is reasonable for you, your employee can apply to Access to Work (opens in new tab) to help with the costs.

Access to Work grants do not have to be paid back, and they will not affect any benefits your employee gets.

You could get expert workplace health support from Occupational Health.
