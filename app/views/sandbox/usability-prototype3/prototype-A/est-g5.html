What to do if your employee has not told you about a health condition or disability

Your employee does not have to give you details of their condition, but there are some things you can do to help.

Reassure your employee

Explain that you will keep health information private, and only use it to make changes to help your employee do their job.

Avoid judgements when you talk about health conditions or disabilities. This is especially important for mental health conditions.

Do not put pressure on your employee to return to work quickly. Come back to the conversation later if they are not ready to talk.

Company culture

It's important to create a culture where employees are happy to talk about about their health. Treating each employee with care will help to create this culture over time.

The Disability Confident scheme (opens in a new tab) can help you become more comfortable with employing and managing people with long-term health conditions and disabilities.

Find more best practice guidance on creating a supportive and enabling workplace on the HSE website (opens in new tab)

Medical evidence and sick pay

You can choose to ask for medical evidence of your employee’s condition if they have been off work for 8 days or more. Examples of medical evidence include a letter from a hospital, or a fit note.

Check more guidance on fit notes (opens in new tab)

You would not have to pay Statutory Sick Pay if they do not provide this evidence.

Agreeing a level of contact when your employee is not in work

You should check any workplace sickness policies or procedures you have in place. These should set the level and type of contact, but you need to agree with your employee how often they will be contacted during their absence. This could be through:

  • calls
  • messages
  • home or workplace visits
  • letters or emails

If an employee requests no contact

As an employer you might need to have some contact to:

  • understand if the reason for this is that the employee is too unwell or incapacitated to maintain contact
  • understand if the absence is due to work-related issues or triggers
  • understand the up-to-date medical position or request a fit note
  • discuss a return to work plan, when appropriate

You could agree to reduce contact or to communicate in writing only for a set period.

Excessive contact could give an employee grounds to complain of harassment under the Protection From Harassment Act 1997 (opens in new tab).
