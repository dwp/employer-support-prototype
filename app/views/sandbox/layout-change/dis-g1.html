Your rights as an employer

To meet your legal obligations you must consider all the options available to you before making any decision about your employees' future with your company.

! Warning As an employer, you have certain obligations towards your employees.

This includes:

  • ensuring their health, safety and welfare at work
  • not discriminating against them
  • putting adjustments in place where reasonable

To support them, you should make sure you have tried all options available, including:

  • making sure adjustments are right for your employee
  • letting your employee know about possible financial help available to them
  • getting expert workplace health support from an Occupational Health service

Protecting yourself and your business

You should make sure that you have followed your legal obligations and duties as an employer.

  • Check your legal obligations in the information and guidance on GOV.UK (opens in new tab)
  • Check information on your duties in protecting disabled employees on the HSE website (opens in new tab)
  • Find out more on the law around workplace adjustments on the HSE website (opens in new tab)

Keeping a record of conversations and meetings

You must have kept a record of conversations or meetings you had with your employee. You should have noted:

  • when you’ve been in touch
  • what you discussed and agreed on
  • any timeframes that were set

The notes could help to protect your business. You might need to show evidence of communication with your employee about their health condition or disability if there is any employment tribunal litigation or as part of the dismissal process.

! Warning You must make sure that you are following the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR).  

Everyone responsible for using personal data must follow data protection laws. You must make sure that your employee's personal data is used properly and lawfully.

Your employee might ask you not to share details of sensitive personal information (such as details of health conditions). You should ask for their consent to share their information, particularly if this is needed to obtain specialist advice.

Check more guidance on the GDPR (opens in new tab)

If you have tried all options

Dismissal should be a last resort. It should only be considered after you have tried all other options available.

It might be fair for you to dismiss your employee if:

  • your employee cannot do their job because reasonable adjustments cannot be made
  • the adjustments you have tried are not suitable or no longer effective

You should check further guidance and, if needed, seek legal advice on:

  • information on your duties in protecting disabled employees on the HSE website (opens in a new tab)
  • avoiding unlawful discrimination when dismissing an employee on Acas website (opens in a new tab)
  • avoiding unlawful discrimination when dismissing an employee on EHRC (Equality and Human Rights Commission) website (opens in a new tab)
